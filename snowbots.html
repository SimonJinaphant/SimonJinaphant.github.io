UBC Snowbots | Student Team

Team photo during the Intelligent Ground Vehicle Competition of 2015


I joined UBC Snowbots during my first week of university in order to meet other like-minded students and get an exposure to the field of robotics and automation. The team comprised of about 60 undergraduate students who are dedicated to the research and development of autonomous ground vehicles. Although my work primarily relates to software development, I also had the pleasure to work with other students who are developing the mechanical and electrical portion.

During my first year with Snowbots I was one of eight members selected to attend the team’s annual competition; this was also the team’s second time returning to the Intelligent Ground Vehicle Competition (IGVC), hosted by Oakland University in Michigan.

We managed to rank 4th in design and 5th in IOP networking challenge, out of the 40 registered teams from around the world!

Technical details and Responsibilities

A complete picture of our 2015 robot: Snowflake

The software portion of our robot is primarily written in C++, using the Robot Operating System (ROS) framework, with some specific portions written in Python and C.

In the vision system we also use OpenCV to provide fast image processing and capturing techniques, which is essential to our performance critical situation, especially when our software executes on a consumer laptop running Ubuntu.

As a member of the software team, I am also responsible for maintaining the codebase shared with over 10 active developers by following the team’s coding standards and resolving version control conflicts.

I am also responsible for upholding safety protocols in a workshop environment, along with handling fragile equipments each worth over $1,000 -including a large LIDAR laser scanner, estimated to be worth around $6,000!

AI: Pathfinding

Due to my prior experience with pathfinding algorithms, I was assigned to AI team to collaborate in the development of the robot’s core autonomous navigation software, here I lead the research and development of various pathfinding algorithms in order to determine a suitable one for navigating a 200 ft by 100 ft outdoor obstacle course.

My current accomplishments include the implementation of static pathfinding algorithms such as A*, along with dynamic ones like D* Lite and its derivation: MT-D*Lite.

An attempt to simulate pathfinding with Gazebo

The reason I chose to focus dynamic pathfinding was because our robot must learn about its surrounding environment as it moves with a limited range of vision, thus producing the problem of dynamic obstacles, or to be more accurate, the problem of additional obstacles at run-time.

Dynamic pathfinding algorithms such as D* and MT-D*Lite operate by producing an initial path and re-computing it, using previous results to minimize computation, when changes are detected -thus making it a suitable solution to our problem.

AI: Mapping

Data from our laser scanner being shown on RVIZ

In addition to pathfinding, the AI team is also responsible for developing the mapping software; here we faced the challenge of concurrently mapping the surrounding environment while the robot moves and discovers more.

We collect our mapping data from the robot’s laser scanner and computer vision system, in addition to integrating the data with the compass in order to localize ourself on the map.

Computer Vision

During the upcoming weeks for competition, I assisted the vision team by profiling the runtime performance of the camera capturing and image stitching software, along with implementing some optimizations which resulted in a performance boost by a factor of 4x in best case scenarios.

I also helped fully integrate our ROS nodes with OpenCV libraries, and developed a proper emergency shutdown technique to resolve memory leak and port re-connection issues, resulting in more productive debugging time.
